## 1. The Time Value of Money (TVM)

The most fundamental rule in finance is: **A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow.**

### Why?

1. **Opportunity Cost:** If you have $100 today, you can invest it (e.g., in a savings account or stock market) and earn interest.
2. **Inflation:** Over time, goods and services become more expensive. $100 today buys more groceries than $100 will five years from now.
3. **Risk (Uncertainty):** A promise to get $100 in ten years has a risk that the person or company might go bankrupt before then.

### The Present Value (PV) Formula

To compare future cash to today's cash, we must perform **discounting**. This "shrinks" future cash flows back into today's dollars:

$$\text{Present Value (PV)} = \frac{\text{Future Value (FV)}}{(1 + r)^t}$$

* Where:
  * $\text{FV}$ = The cash flow in a future year.
  * $r$ = The **Discount Rate** (the cost of capital/interest rate that reflects risk).
  * $t$ = The number of years in the future.

## 2. Free Cash Flow (FCF): The Cash in the Register

In accounting, a company can report high "earnings" or "net profit" on paper, but still have no actual cash in the bank (because the cash is tied up in inventory, unpaid bills, or expensive machinery). 

**Free Cash Flow (FCF)** is the actual, physical cash a company generates after paying for all operations, inventory, taxes, and capital investments (like buying new warehouses or machinery). It is the money that can actually be paid back to investors.

### How it is Calculated (Unlevered FCF)

Unlevered FCF represents the cash generated by the operations of the company, ignoring how it is financed (whether it uses debt or equity):

$$\text{FCF} = \text{Operating Income (EBIT)} \times (1 - \text{Tax Rate}) + \text{D&A} - \Delta\text{NWC} - \text{CapEx}$$

* Where:
  * **EBIT (Earnings Before Interest and Taxes):** Operating profit from core business operations.
  * **D&A (Depreciation & Amortization):** Non-cash accounting write-downs. We add this back because no actual cash left the building.
  * **$\Delta$NWC (Change in Net Working Capital):** Cash tied up in short-term assets (like unpaid customer bills or inventory on shelves).
  * **CapEx (Capital Expenditures):** Cash spent buying or upgrading physical assets (machinery, buildings, computers).

## 3. Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)

WACC is the **Discount Rate ($r$)** we use in the DCF formula. It measures the total risk of the business. 

Think of WACC as the hurdle rate: the average interest rate a company "pays" to its investors (lenders who gave them loans, and shareholders who bought stock) to justify taking the risk of investing in them.

$\text{WACC} = \left(\frac{E}{V} \times R_e\right) + \left(\frac{D}{V} \times R_d \times (1 - t)\right)$

* Where:
  * $E$ = Market Value of Equity (Market Cap).
  * $D$ = Market Value of Debt.
  * $V$ = Total Capital ($E + D$).
  * $R_e$ = Cost of Equity (often calculated using CAPM based on stock volatility/beta).
  * $R_d$ = Cost of Debt (average interest rate paid on loans).
  * $t$ = Corporate Tax Rate (interest on debt is tax-deductible).

### Intuition:

* **High WACC (e.g., 12%):** A high-risk, volatile business (like a biotech startup). Future cash is heavily discounted because it is highly uncertain.
* **Low WACC (e.g., 6%):** A low-risk, steady business (like a municipal utility company). Future cash is highly certain, so we discount it very little.

## 4. Terminal Value (TV) & Gordon Growth Model

We cannot model a company's cash flows year-by-year forever. Instead, we project cash flow year-by-year for an **explicit forecast period** (usually 5 to 10 years), and then assume the company stabilizes and grows at a very slow, steady rate **forever** after that. 

This forever-value is called the **Terminal Value (TV)**.

We calculate Terminal Value using the **Gordon Growth Model**:

$\text{Terminal Value (TV)} = \frac{\text{FCF}_{\text{last}} \times (1 + g_{\text{terminal}})}{\text{WACC} - g_{\text{terminal}}}$

* Where:
  * $\text{FCF}_{\text{last}}$ = The free cash flow generated in the final year of the explicit forecast.
  * $g_{\text{terminal}}$ = The **Terminal Growth Rate** (perpetual growth rate).
    * *Crucial Rule:* $g_{\text{terminal}}$ **must** be lower than the long-term growth rate of the global economy (usually set at 2% to 3%, matching inflation). If it were higher, the company would eventually grow larger than the entire earth's economy!

## 5. Reverse DCF: "Inverting the Story"

Professor Aswath Damodaran (NYU Stern), a world-famous valuation expert, advocates that valuation is a blend of **storytelling and numbers**. 

### The Problem with Normal DCF

In a standard DCF, a analyst makes predictions (e.g., "This company will grow revenue at 20% for 10 years") and outputs an "intrinsic price". The issue is **garbage in, garbage out**: slightly tweaking assumptions can make a stock look incredibly cheap or incredibly expensive. It gives an illusion of precision.

### The Power of Reverse DCF

A Reverse DCF flips the question:

> **"Instead of guessing what the stock *should* be worth, let’s look at its *actual current market price* and work backward to see what business growth and profit margins are required to justify that price."**

### How the Mathematics Works

1. Get the current **Market Enterprise Value** (Market Cap + Net Debt).
2. Set steady, reasonable baseline variables for WACC, Terminal Growth, and FCF Margin.
3. Use a numerical solver (like Brent's Method) to find the unknown variable (e.g., Revenue growth $r$) that makes:
   $$\text{Model Enterprise Value}(r) - \text{Market Enterprise Value} = 0$$
4. Once you have the **Market-Implied Growth Rate**, you evaluate it like a detective:
   * *Example:* If Tesla's current stock price implies it must grow revenues by **35% per year for the next decade** to justify its price, you ask yourself: *"Is it historically plausible for a manufacturing company of this size to maintain a 35% growth rate for 10 years?"* 
   * If the required rate is unrealistically high, the stock is likely **overvalued**. If the required rate is extremely conservative, the stock might be **undervalued**.
